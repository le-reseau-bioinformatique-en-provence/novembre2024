14:15 Talk 1: Study of giant viruses and interacting partners through the lens of omics approaches
Matthieu Legendre (IGS)
Giant viruses, with their exceptional size and complex genomes, stand out from the viral world. Since their serendipitous discovery about 20 years ago, they have been found to be widespread in various environments. These giant viruses engage in complex interactions and competition with various partners within the host unicellular eukaryotic cells they infect, including other giant viruses from different families. Moreover, some giant viruses are themselves parasitized by smaller viruses and mobile genetic elements, forming intricate host-parasite-hyperparasite networks. In this talk, I will present our recent research exploring the diversity, evolution, genomic regulation, and interactions of giant viruses using various omics approaches. This encompasses genomics, metagenomics, epigenomics and transcriptomics, providing a comprehensive understanding of these complex viruses and interacting partners.
15:00 Talk 2: Diversity of Bioluminescent Bacterial Communities in Mesopelagic Fish: Metabarcoding Analyses and Implications for the Biological Carbon Pump
Capucine Lecam-ligier (MIO)
This study focuses on the analysis of microbial diversity in mesopelagic fish sampled during the APERO expedition in the Northeast Atlantic. It highlights the variations in bacterial composition depending on migratory behaviors, bioluminescence, and the feeding habits of the fish. The objective is to understand how these factors influence the role of bioluminescent bacteria in the biological carbon pump.
15:45 Talk 3: Human microbiome proteobacteria: a peep into the molecular dialogue
Jaime Fernandez-Macgregor-Autrique(AFMB/TAGC)
High-throughput quantitative protein-protein interaction methods allow for a systems level perspective of molecular mechanisms. Join us for an exploration of how we've applied this approach to understand how human microbiome proteobacteria establish a molecular dialogue with human cells, through effector proteins they secrete directly into the human cell's cytosol. These bacterial effectors contain conserved structured domains and short linear motifs with which they interact with host proteins and perturb host cellular function.
